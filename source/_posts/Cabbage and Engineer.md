### I returned to Tokyo today

I returned to Tokyo today and back to the apartment where I hadn't lived in months. Today's dinner was barren, consisting only of boiled frozen dumplings, dumpling soup, and boiled carrots with no side dishes. I opened the fridge several times, but there was nothing fresh inside. As I didn't have time, I ended up taking a hot sauce that had been opened for a few months to go with my rice, but it was too spicy. I had to sprinkle some dried nuts and raisins on the rice that I brought back from the airport.

### Cabbage and Engineer

The fridge contained only some expired foods, including dehydrated cabbage, fruit yogurt, and vermicelli, which obviously could not be used for dinner. It suddenly dawned on me that many people who have been working for years are like this fridge. The knowledge and skills of the industry are like the cabbage in the fridge, with a short shelf life. Over time, the cabbage gradually loses its nutrition, and its taste deteriorates.

For example, in a software engineer's career, the "shelf life" of skills and knowledge varies depending on the position and industry, ranging from three years for short ones to twenty years for long ones. Skills that are no longer used, like expired foods, become invalid. Even the ones that were once in the spotlight are now obsolete. And although many other skills are useful, they are no longer popular because they do not meet commercial demands.

So, what are the skills and knowledge that we can use for a lifetime to support ourselves? I believe that habits of learning and recording, patterns of thinking, worldview, and values are very valuable.
